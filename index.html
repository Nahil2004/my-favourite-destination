Ashoka the Buddhist King and Bodh Gaya

Ashoka the Great (304 BCE – 232 BCE)

One of India’s most renowned emperors, ruling the Maurya Dynasty from 268 BCE to 232 BCE. He is best known for spreading Buddhism across Asia and governing with the principles of Dhamma (Dharma).

Early Life & Ascension

  • Son of Emperor Bindusara and grandson of Chandragupta Maurya.
  • Initially, he was a ruthless warrior who expanded the empire through military conquests.
  • After the brutal Kalinga War (261 BCE), in which over 100,000 people died, he deeply regretted the bloodshed and embraced Buddhism.

Adoption of Buddhism

  • Became a devout follower of Buddha’s teachings.
  • Abandoned violent conquests and focused on peace, non-violence, and moral governance.
  • Sent Buddhist missionaries to Sri Lanka, Greece, Egypt, Burma, and Central Asia to spread Buddhism.
  • Built monasteries, stupas, and pillar edicts inscribed with teachings of Buddha and his policies of Dhamma.

Reign & Achievements

  • Governance Based on Dhamma: Promoted religious tolerance, animal welfare, and social justice.
  • Infrastructure Development: Built hospitals, roads, and rest houses for travelers.
  • Ashoka’s Edicts: His policies and messages are recorded on rock and pillar inscriptions found across India, Nepal, Pakistan, and Afghanistan.
  • Expansion of Buddhism: Helped organize the Third Buddhist Council and sent his son Mahinda and daughter Sanghamitta to Sri Lanka to establish Buddhism.

Later Life & Legacy

  • He ruled for nearly 40 years and was one of the greatest Buddhist kings in history.
  • After his death, the Maurya Empire declined, but his legacy endured through Buddhist teachings.
  • Ashoka Chakra, found on India’s national flag, symbolizes his commitment to righteousness.
  • His policies influenced later rulers and Buddhist traditions in Asia.

Ashoka & Buddhism Today

  • Considered a Bodhisattva in Buddhist traditions.
  • His legacy is preserved in ancient inscriptions and stupas like Sanchi Stupa.
  • His Ashoka Pillars are historical symbols of India’s past, with the Lion Capital of Ashoka serving as India’s national emblem.
